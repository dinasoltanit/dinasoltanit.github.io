My Current Research Areas

Plasma Physics And Its Applications In Aerodynamics

Analytical Electrodynamic Modelling Of Dielectric Barrier Discharge Plasma Actuators With Considering The Metamaterial Properties Of The Plasmonic Region

My purpose is to provide a mathematical model to analytically simulate plasma-based flow control actuators based on the electrodynamic considerations of the plasmonic medium. The objective is to develop the model based on fewer assumptions but more flexible for controlling purposes. My ultimate goal is to engineer the dielectric to make it tunable to specific modes to control the magnitude and the direction of the momentum vector produced by the plasma actuator.

This current research is for my Electrical Engineering thesis, in which I am basically investigating the field of flow control. The Aerospace industry is highly investing in active flow control, specifically Plasma Actuators, which show promising potentials for enhancing aerodynamics characteristics. Although such devices have contributed to significant developments, they still have limitations. Providing these actuators with the capability to control the produced momentum vector’s magnitude and direction appears to be a real breakthrough, enabling these actuators to act as various elements such as Flap devices or Spoilers, in addition to their effective usage in the filed of noise control, mostly regarding to the bluff bodies. Being captivated by the discussions on flow control, I dealt with several papers whose aim was to change the direction of the plasma jet by using magnetic fields to gain better-controlling capabilities. Moreover, doing some studies for the Electromagnetics course on Metamaterials, I decided to enquire whether it would be possible to design materials to provide such controlling capabilities for plasma actuators. Doing my research, I seek to build up a numerical model using the governing equations of both sciences, Navier-Stokes, and Maxwell equations, without typical assumptions. Then I would be running a testbench simulation to examine the flow behavior under my defined conditions.

A considerable step regarding this project was completed when I finished my Aerospace Engineering thesis, with the title Computational Modelling Of Dielectric Barrier Discharge Plasma Actuators With Considering The Metamaterial Properties Of The Plasma. The purpose was to provide a computational model for the DBD plasma actuators, including the plasma's metamaterial properties. The model solved the governing Poisson's Equation on the considered domain, treating the plasmonic region as a dielectric media with a calculated permittivity representing the plasmonic region under excitation.
