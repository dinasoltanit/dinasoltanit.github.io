My Current Research Areas

Numerical Modeling and Simulation of The Influence of The Plasma-based Actuators Fluid Flow Fields

Electrohydrodynamic Analysis of Dielectric Barrier Discharge Actuators Based on The Properties of Low Frequency Plasmons

My purpose is to provide a mathematical model to analytically simulate plasma-based flow control actuators based on the properties of Low-Frequency Plasmons. The objective is to develop a model based on fewer assumptions but more flexible for controlling purposes. My ultimate goal is to formulate the influence of the dielectric barrier discharge actuators on fluid flow with fewer assumptions but more flexiblity to make it tunable in terms of the magnitude and the direction of the momentum vector produced by the plasma actuator.

The research I did for obtaining the degree of Bachelor of Science in Aerospace Engineering and for the degree of Bachelor of Science in Electrical Engineering with specialization in Electromagnetics was centered on the purpose mentioned above. Working on my Aerospace Engineering thesis entitled Computational Modelling of Dielectric Barrier Discharge Actuators Based on The Properties of Low-Frequency Plasmons, I studied the dominant phenomenological models for the simulation of dielectric barrier discharge actuators. I then conceptualized the dielectric barrier discharge actuators as a two-layered medium. I implemented the properties of low-frequency plasmons based on the Lorentz Model to simulate the plasma layer of the actuator's structure. Furthermore, I developed a computational model by implementing the properties of low-frequency plasmons based on the Lorentz Model to incorporate the plasma generation and formulated the energy transfer from the plasmonic region to the ambient flow in terms of body force components incorporated into the Navier Stokes equations as the source terms.

Furthermore, with my Electrical Engineering thesis entitled Electrodynamic Analysis of Dielectric Barrier Discharge Actuators Based on The Properties of Low-Frequency Plasmons, I numerically discretized the integral equation relating the electric potential to the charge density on the actuator's electrodes for the structure of a dielectric barrier discharge actuator, based on the Method of Moments. I determined the closed-form spatial Green's Function based on the Complex Images Method for electrostatic fields and the Generalized pencil-of-function numerical algorithm. I then Implemented Finite Difference Approximation to post-process the fields for validation purposes. Based on the proposed algorithm, I computed and analyzed the electric, electric potential, and electric distance fields for varying geometric specs, three different dielectric materials, and varying excitation frequencies and applied voltages. The results are currently under preparation for being published.

Shock Pattern visualization

We visualized the shock wave patterns and the interactions with aerodynamic models based on the schlieren photography. The experiment was based on the flow interaction with a conical, wedge-shaped, and spherical model. Although we got the data for preprocessing purposes, our team is about to renew the Aerospace Engineering Department's Schlieren setup so that we can redo the shock pattern test, validate the results, and analyze the generated patterns.
